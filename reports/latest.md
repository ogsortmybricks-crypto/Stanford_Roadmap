## 2. Official Requirements: GPA, SAT/ACT, Course Rigor

### GPA
- No official minimum GPA, but admitted students average **3.94–3.96 unweighted**
- Stanford evaluates GPA in context of school and course rigor
- Upward trend matters — a junior/senior rebound can offset earlier struggles

### Standardized Tests
- **Test-required** for the Class of 2030 (Fall 2025 applicants) after a test-optional period during COVID
- Accepts **SAT or ACT**; accepts both original and redesigned ACT versions
- Target scores for competitive applicants: **SAT 1500+, ACT 34+**
- Scores are one component of holistic review; academic potential is the primary criterion

> *Source:* Stanford News (June 2024): "Stanford to resume standardized test requirement" — https://news.stanford.edu/stories/2024/06/stanford-to-resume-standardized-test-requirement

### Course Rigor
Stanford's recommended high school preparation (from official admissions guidance):
- **English:** 4 years
- **Mathematics:** 3–4 years (through pre-calculus or calculus)
- **Science:** 3–4 years (including lab sciences)
- **Social Studies/History:** 3–4 years
- **Foreign Language:** 3–4 years of one language
- **Electives:** Courses aligned with intellectual interests

AP, IB, Honors, and dual-enrollment courses signal willingness to challenge yourself. Stanford specifically values coursework aligned with a student's declared area of intellectual interest — an aspiring engineer should take AP Physics and AP Calculus; a humanities student should take AP English, AP History.

> *Source:* Stanford Admission "Preparing for Stanford Academics" page; PrepScholar; CollegeVine; Park Tutoring

## 4. Essays (2025–2026 Prompts)

Stanford's supplemental application includes **five short essays** (50 words each) and **three longer essays** (100–250 words each).

### Short Essays (50 words each — exact prompts vary year to year; examples from 2025–2026):
These typically ask about:
- A thing, idea, or experience you find genuinely compelling
- Your favorite book, movie, piece of music, or creative work
- A community or group you belong to
- Something you've done on your own initiative that isn't on your transcript
- What you want your future college roommate to know about you *(also appears as a long essay)*

### Long Essays (100–250 words each — 2025–2026 confirmed prompts):
1. **Intellectual Curiosity:** "The Stanford community is deeply curious and driven to learn in and out of the classroom. Reflect on an idea or experience that makes you genuinely excited about learning."
2. **Future Roommate:** "Write a note to your future roommate that reveals something about you or that will help your roommate—and us—get to know you better."
3. **Distinctive Contribution:** "Please describe what aspects of your life experiences, interests and character would help you make a distinctive contribution as an undergraduate to Stanford University."

### Essay Strategy Notes
- **Roommate essay:** Shows personality, voice, humor, warmth — be specific and human, not generic
- **Intellectual curiosity essay:** Must reference a *genuine* intellectual spark, not what looks impressive
- **Distinctive contribution:** Tie your specific background, perspective, or skill to what you'll add to Stanford's community
- Avoid: listing achievements, writing what you think admissions wants to hear, vague statements about "making the world a better place"

> *Sources:* AdmitStudio; Accepted.com blog; Selective Admissions; CollegeVine; Ivy Coach supplemental essay guides

## 6. Financial Aid

Stanford's financial aid program is among the most generous in the country:

| Income Bracket | Expected Family Contribution |
|---|---|
| Below ~$75,000/year | $0 (full tuition, room, and board covered) |
| ~$75,000–$150,000 | Typically $0 tuition (some contribution to other costs) |
| Above $150,000 | Sliding scale; need-based grants still available |

- **No loans** in Stanford's need-based aid packages — aid is grants only
- ~70% of Stanford undergraduates receive some form of financial assistance
- Approximately 20% of families pay nothing to attend

### Required Financial Aid Documents
- **CSS Profile** (institutional aid — required for most need-based aid)
- **FAFSA** (federal/state aid)
- **IDOC** (tax return documents through College Board)

> *Sources:* Stanford Financial Aid official pages (financialaid.stanford.edu); Stanford FAQ; CollegeAdvisor; Scholarships360

## 7. What Distinguishes Accepted Applicants

### Academic Excellence (Necessary But Not Sufficient)
- Near-perfect GPA in the most rigorous courses available at your school
- SAT 1500+ or ACT 34+ (Class of 2030 and beyond, required)
- AP/IB scores of 4s and 5s, especially in areas of focus
- Strong upward trajectory; no significant unexplained grade drops

### "Spike" — Depth Over Breadth
Admitted students typically have a **defining area of excellence** ("spike"), not a laundry list of clubs:
- An aspiring scientist who has conducted original research and published or presented findings
- A musician who has performed at Carnegie Hall or released original compositions
- An entrepreneur who built a real business with revenue
- A writer who has published in notable outlets
- An athlete competing at elite (Olympic, national) level

Stanford is explicitly looking for **"Tier 1" extracurriculars**: winning the Regeneron Science Talent Search, qualifying for USAMO, earning national recognition in the arts. These are rare but represent the bar for the most competitive applicants.

### Leadership With Measurable Impact
- Not just holding a title, but demonstrating what *changed* because of your leadership
- Starting organizations, launching programs, mentoring others
- Impact visible in the community beyond the school

### Intellectual Curiosity Demonstrated Outside Class
- Students who learn on their own initiative — reading papers, pursuing online courses, attending lectures
- Independent research, even without formal affiliation
- Intellectual passion that connects academic interests to real-world problems

### Essays That Sound Like a Real Person
- Authentic voice and specific detail
- The "Future Roommate" essay is often where personality breaks through
- Avoid generic "I want to change the world" framing; show *how* and *why*

### Recommendation Letters That Go Beyond "Good Student"
- Letters that describe specific classroom moments, intellectual breakthroughs, or character insights
- Teachers who've seen you struggle and persist, not just excel effortlessly

### What Gets Rejected (Common Patterns)
- Strong academics with generic, "safe" activities (NHS, student council, a sport, volunteering) and no standout achievement
- Essays that are polished but don't reveal a real person
- "Resume-dump" essays that repeat the activities list
- Last-minute joining of clubs in 11th/12th grade (called "Junior Joiners")
- Impressive-sounding activities without demonstrated personal passion or growth

> *Cross-referenced:* CollegeVine; Shemmassian Consulting; Koppelman Group; Ingenius Prep; College Essay Guy; AdmitStudio

## 8. Grade-by-Grade Preparation Roadmap

### 8th Grade (Pre-High School)
- **Academics:** Ensure strong math foundation (pre-algebra/algebra); request honors/advanced placement in 9th grade
- **Mindset:** Start exploring intellectual interests broadly — science camps, coding, creative writing, arts, debate
- **Test:** Take PSAT 8/9 (optional, but useful baseline)

---

### 9th Grade (Freshman Year)
**Goal:** Build a strong academic foundation; begin exploring your "spike"

**Academics:**
- Take Honors or accelerated courses where available
- Establish straight-A habits from day one — 9th grade GPA counts
- Build relationships with teachers early (future recommenders)

**Extracurriculars:**
- Join 2–3 activities that genuinely interest you — don't over-extend
- Try things you've never done; it's OK to quit something that doesn't fit
- Start one activity that connects to a potential long-term interest (coding club, debate, theater, research society)

**Community/Leadership:**
- Volunteer in a meaningful cause (not just for hours — something personal)
- Look for any leadership opportunities within your activities

**Test Prep:**
- Take PSAT 8/9 if offered — useful diagnostic, not high-stakes
- Begin light SAT/ACT familiarity (Khan Academy SAT prep is free)

**Summer:**
- Academic enrichment program, local volunteer work, or an exploratory project
- If interested in STEM: local university lab volunteer, science Olympiad, coding projects

---

### 10th Grade (Sophomore Year)
**Goal:** Narrow focus, deepen commitment, begin test preparation seriously

**Academics:**
- Push into AP or IB coursework in your strongest subject areas
- Aim for all A's; if a subject is difficult, get tutoring early
- Choose electives that align with your developing intellectual identity

**Extracurriculars:**
- Reduce breadth: commit deeply to 2–3 core activities
- Shift from *participation* to *contribution*: lead a project, mentor a peer, start a program
- Begin pursuing activities outside school: regional competitions, community organizations

**Research & Projects:**
- Reach out to a local university professor in your area of interest for a lab visit or informal mentorship
- Start a small independent project — it doesn't need to win awards yet, but build the habit of inquiry
- Consider RSI, PRIMES, or other competitive summer research programs (apply in 10th grade fall)

**Test Prep:**
- Take PSAT 10 in October (practice for junior year's National Merit PSAT)
- Take the PreACT
- Run a diagnostic SAT and ACT to determine which format suits you better
- Begin structured prep: target SAT 1400+ or ACT 31+ by end of sophomore year

**Summer:**
- Apply to competitive summer programs: Stanford Pre-Collegiate, MIT PRIMES, RSI (highly selective), Governor's School, academic camps in your field
- Pursue a project with real deliverables (a GitHub repo, an essay, an art portfolio piece)

---

### 11th Grade (Junior Year)
**Goal:** Peak academic performance, significant extracurricular achievements, standardized testing

This is the most important academic year. Colleges scrutinize junior-year grades and course rigor most heavily.

**Academics:**
- Most rigorous course load yet — multiple APs, IB courses, or dual enrollment
- Maintain or improve GPA
- If interested in STEM: AP Calculus BC, AP Physics C, AP Chemistry/Biology
- If humanities: AP English Language, AP US History, AP Government

**Standardized Testing:**
- **October:** Take PSAT/NMSQT (National Merit qualifying test — scores used for National Merit Scholarship)
- **March/May/June:** Take SAT for the first time
- **April/June:** Take ACT if considering it as an alternative
- Target: **SAT 1500+ or ACT 34+** for Stanford competitiveness
- Use College Board's free Khan Academy prep for SAT

**Extracurriculars:**
- Achieve your most significant milestones this year: national competition results, research presentations, publications, performances
- Take on top leadership positions in your core activities
- If doing research, aim for a tangible output (paper, poster, competition entry)
- Athletic recruiting: if pursuing D1/D3 athletics, this is the key contact year with coaches

**College Prep:**
- Begin college list research (fall/spring)
- Visit campuses if possible
- Begin thinking about essay themes — journal about experiences and ideas that matter to you
- Identify 2–3 teachers for recommendations (ask at the end of junior year or start of senior year)

**Summer (Between 11th and 12th):**
- Write essay drafts (aim to have drafts done before school starts)
- Pursue a capstone project, internship, research program, or entrepreneurial venture
- Retake SAT/ACT if needed

---

### 12th Grade (Senior Year)
**Goal:** Complete applications; finish strong academically

**Summer Before 12th:**
- Write all essay drafts
- Finalize college list (Stanford + 8–12 total schools)
- Request teacher recommendations (July/August — give teachers at least 6–8 weeks)

**Fall Semester:**
- **August–October:** Finalize and polish applications and essays
- **October:** Final SAT/ACT retake if needed (October date for REA applicants)
- **November 1:** Submit REA application (if applying Early)
- **November 15:** Submit CSS Profile for financial aid (REA priority)
- **Mid-December:** Receive REA decision
- **January 5:** Submit Regular Decision application
- **February 15:** Submit FAFSA; submit Mid-Year Report

**Spring:**
- Continue strong academic performance (Stanford can rescind admission for grade drops)
- **May 1:** Enrollment deposit deadline

## 9. Test Prep Timeline

| Timeframe | Action |
|---|---|
| 8th grade | PSAT 8/9 (diagnostic baseline) |
| 9th grade | Light SAT/ACT exposure; Khan Academy familiarity |
| 10th grade (October) | PSAT 10 (practice) |
| 10th grade (fall–spring) | Diagnostic full-length SAT and ACT; pick preferred test |
| 10th grade (spring–summer) | Begin structured prep for chosen test |
| 11th grade (October) | **PSAT/NMSQT** (National Merit qualifying — must score in top ~1% of state) |
| 11th grade (March) | First official SAT |
| 11th grade (May/June) | Second SAT attempt or first ACT attempt |
| 11th grade (summer) | Targeted prep on weaknesses; retake planning |
| 12th grade (August/September) | Optional retake if needed |
| 12th grade (October) | **Final attempt** (if applying REA, this is the last feasible SAT/ACT) |

### National Merit Scholarship (PSAT/NMSQT)
- Taken in October of **junior year**
- Top scorers (~16,000 nationally) become Semifinalists; ~15,000 become Finalists
- National Merit Finalist is a meaningful credential on applications
- State-specific cutoffs vary; top states (CA, NY, TX, MA) require approximately 220–223 Selection Index

## 10. Extracurricular, Research & Leadership Milestones by Year

### Tier System for Activities
- **Tier 1 (Extremely Rare):** National/international competition winner (Regeneron STS, USAMO, Intel ISEF top awards, Jack Kent Cooke Young Artist, Olympic/national athletic achievement, major publishing credit, prestigious fellowship)
- **Tier 2 (Very Strong):** State/regional competition winner; published research; founded an organization with measurable impact; significant arts recognition
- **Tier 3 (Solid):** School leadership (president, captain); strong performance in competitive activities; sustained community service
- **Tier 4 (Common):** Club member, participant-level activities, generic volunteering

Stanford's most competitive applicants have at least one Tier 1 or Tier 2 achievement. Most qualified-but-rejected applicants have only Tier 3–4.

### By Year

**9th Grade:**
- Join activities; identify 1–2 potential "spike" areas
- Begin accumulating hours/experience in core interest
- Take on any available leadership (committee chair, team captain in JV)

**10th Grade:**
- Deepen in core activities; let secondary activities fade
- Compete at regional level in core interest
- Begin independent project or research exploration
- Take on a meaningful community service role (not just hours)
- Apply to selective summer programs (RSI, PRIMES, SSTP, Governor's School)

**11th Grade:**
- Compete at state or national level
- Publish, present, or achieve formal recognition in core area
- Hold top leadership role (president, founder, principal investigator)
- Mentor or teach younger students
- Complete significant independent research or creative project

**12th Grade:**
- Continue and complete ongoing commitments
- Achieve any final milestones (competition results, publications, performances)
- Maintain community service investment
- Demonstrate follow-through: don't abandon activities once applications are submitted

### Key Extracurricular Categories and Competitive Milestones

| Category | Tier 1 Milestone | Tier 2 Milestone |
|---|---|---|
| STEM/Research | Regeneron STS, ISEF grand prize, USAMO qualifier | ISEF finalist, published paper, RSI alumni |
| Math | USAMO qualifier (top ~500 nationally) | AIME qualifier (top ~5%) |
| Writing/Journalism | Published in major outlet, book published | Award-winning school paper, regional recognition |
| Music | Carnegie Hall, major competition winner | State All-State, recognized youth ensemble |
| Visual Arts | National gallery showing, major prize | Regional exhibition, AP Art 5, portfolio recognition |
| Community Service | Founded nonprofit with 100+ beneficiaries | Sustained leadership in local org, measurable impact |
| Entrepreneurship | Real business with revenue/users | Launched product or app with users |
| Athletics | Olympic trials, national champion | State champion, recruited D1 prospect |
