## 1. Official Requirements

### GPA
- **Average admitted GPA:** 3.94 (2024–25 cycle)
- **73.3%** of admitted students reported a 4.0 GPA
- Stanford does not publish a minimum GPA requirement; context (course rigor, school grading scale) matters enormously
- **Typical AP/IB load:** 8–12 AP or IB courses across four years
- Stanford recommends mastery across **five core areas**: English, Mathematics, Social Studies, Science, and World Language

> **Cross-reference:** Confirmed by CollegeVine (blog.collegevine.com) and PrepScholar (blog.prepscholar.com)

### Standardized Tests (SAT/ACT)
| Metric | Range |
|--------|-------|
| SAT Middle 50% | 1510–1570 |
| ACT Middle 50% | 34–35 |

- **IMPORTANT POLICY CHANGE:** Beginning with the **2025–26 application cycle (Class of 2030)**, Stanford **reinstated a standardized testing requirement**. The university is no longer test-optional.
- Both SAT and ACT are accepted; no preference stated
- No minimum score guarantees admission; scores are reviewed in holistic context
- Superscoring: Stanford considers the highest section scores across multiple test dates

> **Sources:** Stanford Standardized Testing page (admission.stanford.edu/apply/first-year/testing.html, accessed via search 2026-04); CollegeVine 2025; AdmissionSight 2026

### Coursework Rigor
Stanford explicitly values students who take the **most rigorous courses available** at their school:
- 4 years English
- 4 years mathematics (through calculus preferred for STEM)
- 3–4 years science (including lab sciences)
- 3–4 years social studies/history
- 3–4 years of the same world language
- Additional coursework in arts/music recommended

## 2. Application Components

Stanford accepts both the **Common Application** and the **Coalition Application**.

### Required Components
| Component | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| Application form | Common App or Coalition App |
| High school transcript | Official, sent directly |
| Standardized test scores | SAT or ACT required (starting Class of 2030) |
| School counselor report | Required |
| Two teacher recommendations | Core academic subjects (English, math, science, history, or world language); ideally 11th or 12th grade; 10th-grade OK if course was AP/IB level |
| Essays | Three short essays + short-answer questions (see below) |
| Activities list | Up to 10 activities on Common App |
| Application fee | $90 (fee waivers available) |

### Essays & Short Answers
Stanford's writing supplements include:
- **Three short essays** (typically 250 words each) on topics such as:
  - What matters to you, and why
  - What excites your intellectual curiosity
  - How you see yourself contributing to Stanford
- **Short-answer questions** (approximately 50 words each), which in recent cycles have asked about:
  - A challenge you overcame
  - A topic or idea you find genuinely interesting
  - Something unusual about you
  - A community you belong to
  - What you would do with a free afternoon

Essays are described by admissions officers as among the **most important** parts of the Stanford application — they are looking for authentic voice, intellectual vitality, and genuine self-reflection, not polished "college essay" clichés.

### Optional/Additional Components
- One additional recommendation (e.g., coach, mentor, employer) — use only if genuinely adds new dimension
- Arts portfolio (for students with significant visual art, music, dance, or film achievement)

## 5. What Top Admitted Applicants Do — By High School Year

### Freshmen (Grade 9)
- Begin rigorous coursework (pre-AP, honors) across all five core areas
- Explore 2–3 extracurricular areas to discover genuine passions
- Join clubs, teams, or community activities — commitment over quantity
- Begin reading broadly and pursuing intellectual interests outside school
- No need for formal SAT prep yet; focus on academic foundation

### Sophomores (Grade 10)
- Begin to **narrow extracurricular focus** — move from exploration to deepening
- Take AP/IB courses where available; demonstrate ability to handle college-level work
- Take PSAT 10 (October) for practice
- Begin pursuing leadership within activities you are committed to
- Consider independent projects, research, or creative work related to a passion area
- If interested in research, seek summer programs (local university programs, RSI, SSP)

### Juniors (Grade 11)
- **Most critical academic year** — rigorous coursework, high GPA especially important
- Take PSAT/NMSQT (October) — gateway to National Merit Scholarship recognition
- Take **SAT/ACT for the first time** (spring of junior year recommended; fall backup)
- Demonstrate **clear leadership** in 1–2 primary activities
- Pursue summer research, internships, competitions, or other high-impact experiences
- Begin identifying teachers for recommendation letters (ask end of junior year or early senior year)
- Begin drafting Common App essay over the summer after junior year

### Seniors (Grade 12)
- Continue rigorous coursework — **do not senioritis-coast**; Stanford verifies final grades
- Retake SAT/ACT if needed (September/October)
- Finalize application, essays, and activity descriptions
- **REA deadline: November 1**; **RD deadline: January 5**
- Submit CSS Profile and IDOC by November 15 for early financial aid notice
- Continue deepening primary extracurricular commitments through senior year

## 6. Long-Term Preparation Strategies by Grade Level

### Grade 8 (Pre-High School)
- Develop strong study habits and time management
- Explore a range of interests — music, sports, coding, writing, science, etc.
- Take the most rigorous math available (Algebra I or Algebra II if ready)
- Read widely and develop genuine intellectual curiosity

### Grade 9
**Academic focus:** Foundational rigor
- Aim for A/A+ in all courses; every grade counts
- Take honors/pre-AP where offered; save AP for 10th–12th
- Begin a world language if not already started (continuity matters)

**Extracurricular focus:** Broad exploration
- Try multiple activities; don't worry about "the spike" yet
- Attend clubs, camps, or programs in areas of interest

**Tests:** No formal prep needed; familiarize yourself with test structure

### Grade 10
**Academic focus:** First AP/IB courses
- Target AP Human Geography, AP European History, AP Computer Science Principles, or AP Language and Composition as "entry-level" APs
- Maintain top grades under increased rigor

**Extracurricular focus:** Begin narrowing
- Identify 2–3 activities you want to deepen
- Seek leadership opportunities within existing activities

**Tests:** Take PSAT 10 in October for practice; analyze results

### Grade 11
**Academic focus:** Peak rigor
- Take the most demanding course load your school offers (typically 4–5 APs for strong candidates)
- Target As in all courses; a dip junior year is a red flag

**Extracurricular focus:** Leadership and impact
- Hold a leadership position in primary activity
- Begin meaningful community impact (not box-checking service)
- Apply to selective summer programs: Research Science Institute (RSI), Stanford EPGY, PRIMES, Regeneron competitions, prestigious internships

**Tests:**
- October: PSAT/NMSQT (National Merit cutoff)
- March/April/May: First SAT or ACT attempt
- June: Retake if needed

### Grade 12
**Academic focus:** Maintain rigor; no grade collapse
- Continue AP/IB course load
- Strong mid-year grades matter (submitted to Stanford)

**Extracurricular focus:** Culminate and demonstrate impact
- Bring primary activity to a high point (competition, publication, performance, project completion)
- Document achievements for activity descriptions

**Application deadlines:**
- REA: November 1 | RD: January 5

## 7. Extracurriculars, Research, Leadership & Community Service Milestones

### The "Spike" vs. "Well-Rounded" Debate
Stanford — and elite admissions broadly — has shifted toward valuing **depth and demonstrated excellence** in a focused area over superficial participation in many clubs. The ideal applicant profile is often described as "spiky": exceptional in one or two areas, with broad enough curiosity to thrive at a research university.

### Activity Tiers (From Most to Least Impressive)
| Tier | Examples |
|------|---------|
| **Tier 1** | National/international competition winner (Regeneron STS, ISEF, USAMO, USA Biology/Physics/Chemistry Olympiad, Intel/Google Science Fair); published research; professional-level performance (symphony, nationally ranked athlete); significant entrepreneurial impact |
| **Tier 2** | State-level competition winner; significant leadership in regional organization; research with university faculty; meaningful creative publication or exhibit; Eagle Scout/Gold Award with substantive project |
| **Tier 3** | School-level leadership (student government president, newspaper editor, varsity captain); AP subject mastery; community service organization founder; significant tutoring or mentorship program |
| **Tier 4** | Club membership; varsity letter; community service volunteer; part-time work |

### Key Milestones by Year
| Year | Milestone Goal |
|------|---------------|
| 9th | Join 2–3 activities; establish initial commitment |
| 10th | Take leadership role; enter first regional competition or start independent project |
| 11th | State-level achievement or published/presented research; significant leadership position; meaningful community impact |
| 12th | Peak achievement; culminating project, competition, performance, or publication |

### Community Service
Stanford does not simply count service hours. They look for:
- **Sustained, meaningful involvement** (not one-day volunteering events)
- **Leadership and initiative** (founding a drive, organizing peers, creating a program)
- **Genuine connection** to your interests and community

### Research Pathways
- **9th–10th grade:** Science fair projects (regional/state level), coding projects, writing portfolios
- **11th grade:** Contact local university faculty for research opportunities; apply to RSI (Research Science Institute), PRIMES (MIT), Clark Scholars, or similar summer research programs
- **12th grade (pre-application):** Submit papers, competition entries, or presentations that demonstrate original work

## 8. Test Prep Timeline (PSAT, SAT/ACT)

### Overview
Stanford's test policy for **Class of 2030 (applying fall 2025)** and beyond: **SAT or ACT required.** No longer test-optional.

### Recommended Timeline
| Grade | Test | Timing | Goal |
|-------|------|--------|------|
| 8th | None | — | Build strong math/reading foundation |
| 9th | PSAT 8/9 (optional) | Fall | Baseline diagnostic only |
| 10th | PSAT 10 | October | Practice; identify weak areas |
| 11th | PSAT/NMSQT | October | National Merit eligibility (Selection Index ~216+ for most states) |
| 11th | SAT (first attempt) | March, May, or June | Aim for 1500+ first attempt |
| 11th | SAT/ACT (retake if needed) | August, October, November | Target 1550+ |
| 12th | SAT/ACT (final retake if needed) | August or October | Finalize score |

### Score Targets
- **SAT:** 1550 is a reasonable target to be competitive; 1570+ puts you in the top quartile of Stanford admits
- **ACT:** 35+ is competitive; 36 is ideal
- Below 1500 SAT / 34 ACT does not disqualify, but it adds pressure on other components

### Test Prep Strategies
1. **Diagnostic first:** Take a full, timed practice test to establish a baseline
2. **Identify weak areas:** Focus prep on specific question types, not general review
3. **Official materials:** Khan Academy SAT prep (free, official partnership) is highly effective for SAT; ACT official practice tests for ACT
4. **Timing:** Allow 3–6 months of structured prep before first official attempt
5. **Superscoring:** Stanford superscore; report your best section scores from multiple test dates

## 9. What Distinguishes Accepted from Rejected Applicants

Based on patterns across admissions data, expert analysis, and self-reported applicant experiences, the following factors most reliably differentiate admitted Stanford students:

### Academic Differentiation
| Accepted | Rejected |
|---------|---------|
| 4.0 GPA in most rigorous courses available | 3.8–3.9 GPA with easier course load |
| 8–12 AP/IB courses; mostly 5s and 4s | Fewer APs; scores of 3 or below |
| Consistent excellence; no significant dips | Grade dip in junior year |
| Above 1560 SAT / 35 ACT | Below 1500 SAT / 34 ACT |

### Extracurricular Differentiation
| Accepted | Rejected |
|---------|---------|
| Deep excellence in 1–2 areas (Tier 1–2 achievement) | Broad participation in 8–10 activities (Tier 4 across the board) |
| Clear narrative connecting activities to identity/goals | Activities look like resume-padding |
| Founded or led something with tangible impact | Held titles without demonstrable effect |
| Multi-year, escalating commitment | One-year surge senior year |
| Nationally competitive achievement | School-only achievement |

### Essay Differentiation
| Accepted | Rejected |
|---------|---------|
| Authentic, specific, surprising voice | Generic or "what they want to hear" tone |
| Reveals genuine intellectual curiosity | Lists accomplishments already in resume |
| Shows how applicant thinks, not just what they've done | Surface-level reflection |
| Consistent narrative across essays and application | Contradictory or inconsistent self-presentation |

### The "Intellectual Vitality" Factor
Stanford explicitly cites **intellectual vitality** as a key quality they seek — the ability to pursue ideas beyond what's required, to think deeply, and to demonstrate genuine passion for learning. This often shows up in:
- Independent research or creative projects
- Engagement with ideas in essays that goes beyond the classroom
- Recommendations that describe a student's intellectual impact on their community

### Holistic Context
Stanford considers every application in context: the strength of your school's course offerings, your family background, geographic location, socioeconomic circumstances, and more. A 3.8 GPA at a competitive magnet school may be viewed differently than a 3.8 at a school with limited AP offerings.
