## 2. Official Requirements: GPA, Test Scores, Coursework
 
### GPA
- **Average GPA (Class of 2028):** 3.94 (unweighted equivalent)
- Stanford does not publish a minimum GPA; the expectation is near-perfect grades in the most rigorous curriculum available
### SAT / ACT (Test-Required as of 2025–2026)
Stanford reinstated its standardized testing requirement in June 2024. Submitting scores is **mandatory** for the 2025–2026 cycle and beyond; hardship waivers are available only under exceptional circumstances.
 
| Test | Middle 50% (Class of 2028) |
|---|---|
| SAT | 1510–1570 |
| ACT | 34–35 |
| SAT Average | ~1540 |
 
- Students who scored 1580–1600 (SAT) or 36 (ACT) are still regularly rejected — scores are necessary but not sufficient.
**Sources:** [Stanford Admissions – Standardized Testing](https://admission.stanford.edu/apply/first-year/testing.html), [Stanford News – Test Requirement Reinstated](https://news.stanford.edu/stories/2024/06/stanford-to-resume-standardized-test-requirement), [Research.com – Stanford Admissions 2026](https://research.com/best-colleges/stanford-university/admissions), [AdmitStudio – SAT Analysis Stanford 2025](https://admitstudio.com/undergraduate-articles/sat-analysis-stanford-2025/)
 
### Coursework Rigor
- Rigor of secondary curriculum is rated **"very important"** in Stanford's holistic review
- Expected: 4 years English, 4 years math (through calculus), 3–4 years lab science, 3–4 years social studies, 3–4 years foreign language
- AP, IB, or dual-enrollment courses are strongly recommended wherever available
- Taking the hardest courses your school offers — and earning excellent grades — matters more than grade inflation in easier courses
**Sources:** [Stanford News – Admissions Criteria 2025](https://news.stanford.edu/stories/2025/07/admissions-criteria-application-period), [Holistic Admission – Stanford](https://admission.stanford.edu/apply/overview/index.html)

## 3. Application Components: Essays, Recommendations, Activities
 
### Platform
- Common Application or Coalition Application
- Stanford Supplemental Questions (required)
### Essays
 
**Five Short Questions (50 words each):**
1. What is the most significant challenge that society faces today?
2. Briefly elaborate on one of your extracurricular activities, a job you hold, or responsibilities you have for your family.
3. List five things that are important to you.
4. What is the most significant challenge that you have faced and what did you learn from it?
5. (Additional short question varies by cycle — check the official application for the exact fifth prompt)
**Three Essays (100–250 words each):**
1. *The Stanford community is deeply curious and driven to learn in and out of the classroom. Reflect on an idea or experience that makes you genuinely excited about learning.*
2. *Virtually all of Stanford's undergraduates live on campus. Write a note to your future roommate that reveals something about you or that will help your roommate—and us—get to know you better.*
3. *Please describe what aspects of your life experiences, interests and character would help you make a distinctive contribution as an undergraduate to Stanford University.*
**Essay Strategy:**
- Essays should be specific, personal, and authentic — avoid clichés and generic "I want to change the world" narratives
- The roommate letter is an opportunity for genuine personality and humor; treat it like a real letter, not a formal essay
- The "distinctive contribution" essay is where you make the case for why Stanford needs you specifically
**Sources:** [Selective Admissions – Stanford Essays 2025-2026](https://www.selectiveadmissions.com/blog-posts/stanford-supplemental-essay-prompts-2025-2026), [AdmitStudio – Stanford Essay Guidance 2025](https://admitstudio.com/undergraduate-articles/stanford-essay-guidance-2025/), [CollegeVine – How to Write Stanford Essays](https://blog.collegevine.com/how-to-write-the-stanford-university-essays), [Accepted.com – Tips for Stanford Essays](https://blog.accepted.com/tips-for-answering-the-2025-2026-stanford-supplemental-essay-prompts/)
 
### Letters of Recommendation
- **2 teacher recommendations** (from 11th- or 12th-grade teachers preferred; 10th-grade acceptable if the course was advanced)
  - Recommended subjects: English, math, science, history, or world language
- **1 school counselor recommendation** (required, includes school report)
- **Optional:** Additional recommendation from a peer, employer, coach, or mentor (use sparingly — only if it adds a genuinely new dimension)
### Activities List
- Up to 10 extracurricular activities listed in order of importance
- Include hours/week, weeks/year, positions held
- Brief description of impact and personal significance
- Quality and depth trump quantity
### Optional Arts Portfolio
Available for highly accomplished students in: art practice, dance, music, or theater and performance. Only submit if you have genuine pre-professional-level achievement.

- Stanford's REA is **restrictive**: you may not apply Early Decision or Early Action to any other private U.S. college or university while applying REA to Stanford

- Missing financial aid deadlines does not affect admission decisions but may delay or reduce your aid package

## 5. What Top Admitted Applicants Do
 
### The "Spike" vs. "Well-Rounded" Debate
Stanford does not want a generic well-rounded student — it builds a well-rounded *class*. Admitted applicants typically have a distinctive **"spike"**: a deep, demonstrable passion in one or two areas where they have achieved something exceptional.
 
### Tier 1 Extracurricular Achievements (Extremely Competitive)
- Regeneron Science Talent Search finalist or winner
- Intel/ISEF national award winner
- USA(J)MO qualifier or IMO team member
- National Merit Scholar (Semifinalist at minimum)
- Published research (peer-reviewed or credible preprint)
- State or national champion in a sport, debate, or fine arts
- Founded a nonprofit with measurable community impact
- Competitive music: symphony-level performance, major competition winner
- Congressional app challenge finalist, Presidential Scholar nominee
### What Actually Moves the Needle
- **Self-directed intellectual work**: Starting a project nobody asked you to start (independent research, a business, a community initiative, a publication)
- **Long-term commitment**: Multi-year involvement in 1–2 activities rather than dabbling in 10
- **Genuine impact**: Evidence that your participation changed something — a club you grew, a community problem you solved, a skill you taught others
- **Authentic intellectual curiosity**: Cold-emailing professors, reading beyond the curriculum, pursuing ideas out of genuine interest

## 6. Year-by-Year High School Preparation Strategy
 
### 9th Grade (Freshman Year)
**Academic:**
- Take the most challenging courses your school offers (Honors where available)
- Establish strong study habits — a GPA slip in 9th grade can be hard to recover
- Begin exploring foreign language if not already (aim for 4 years total)
**Extracurriculars:**
- Join 2–3 clubs aligned with genuine interests; avoid joining everything
- Identify 1–2 potential "spike" activities to develop over 4 years
- Begin community service — consistency matters more than hours logged
**Testing:**
- Take PSAT 8/9 (if offered at your school) for baseline awareness — no preparation needed
- No pressure to take SAT/ACT yet
**Long-term:**
- Start reading widely; develop intellectual curiosity outside of school
- Explore summer programs (enrichment, not necessarily resume-padding)
---
 
### 10th Grade (Sophomore Year)
**Academic:**
- Move into AP or IB courses in your strongest subjects
- Maintain all-A grades while increasing rigor
- Begin thinking about potential majors/fields of interest
**Extracurriculars:**
- Deepen commitment to 1–2 core activities; take on minor leadership (team captain, club officer)
- Begin independent projects related to your spike (coding a small app, writing for publication, etc.)
- Pursue a meaningful summer opportunity (research program, internship, intensive course)
**Testing:**
- Take PSAT/NMSQT in October (not for National Merit — that's 11th grade — but for practice)
- Begin light SAT/ACT familiarization in spring; take a full practice test
**Community Service:**
- Identify a cause you genuinely care about; begin consistent involvement
---
 
### 11th Grade (Junior Year) — Most Critical Year
**Academic:**
- Maximum AP/IB load (consistent with your school's offerings)
- This year's grades are the last full-year grades colleges see before decision
- Target 5s on AP exams in your primary subject areas
**Extracurriculars:**
- Achieve meaningful leadership positions (president, founder, section principal, team captain)
- Launch independent project, research initiative, or business if not already
- Pursue summer research (university REU programs, hospital/lab internships, independent study)
- Apply for competitions, scholarships, and recognition programs
**Testing:**
- Take PSAT/NMSQT in October (this is the National Merit qualifying test)
- Take SAT in March, May, or June; ACT in April or June
- Aim for your target score in one or two sittings
- Use Khan Academy (SAT) or reputable ACT prep materials
**College Research:**
- Begin researching schools in earnest; visit Stanford if possible
- Identify potential essay themes and begin brainstorming
- Request teacher recommendations at the end of junior year (give teachers the summer)
---
 
### 12th Grade (Senior Year)
**Academic:**
- Maintain rigor — senioritis is a real risk and Stanford can rescind offers for grade drops
- Take the most advanced courses available; don't coast
**Extracurriculars:**
- Consolidate your narrative: your senior-year activities should reinforce your application story
- Pursue any final competitions or milestones before application deadlines
**Testing:**
- Retake SAT/ACT in August/September if needed to improve scores
- Do not test more than 3 times total
**Application Timeline:**
- August–September: Finalize essay drafts; complete activity descriptions
- October: Submit REA application (deadline November 1)
- November–December: Complete RD applications if needed (deadline January 5)
- December: Receive REA decision
- March–April: Receive RD decisions

| Grade | Test | Timing | Purpose |

| 8th–9th | PSAT 8/9 | October (school-administered) | Baseline diagnostic only |
| 10th | PSAT/NMSQT | October | Practice for junior-year NM qualifying test |
| 11th | PSAT/NMSQT | October | **National Merit Scholarship qualifying exam** |
| 11th | SAT | March, May, or June | First official sitting |
| 11th | ACT | April or June | Alternative or supplement to SAT |
| 12th | SAT/ACT | August or September | Final retake if needed |
 
**Key Principles:**
- Stanford recommends taking SAT or ACT **no more than 3 times**
- Prep with official College Board materials (Khan Academy for SAT is free and excellent)
- Begin serious prep 3–4 months before first sitting
- For Stanford, target SAT 1550+ / ACT 35+; the middle 50% range is 1510–1570 / 34–35
- A score in the top quarter (1570+ SAT) puts you in the competitive zone; scores below 1500 are a meaningful obstacle

## 8. What Distinguishes Accepted from Rejected Applicants
 
### Quantitative Threshold
Rejected applicants often have perfect or near-perfect stats. A 4.0 GPA and 1600 SAT do not guarantee admission — approximately 75% of applicants with those scores are still rejected. Stats get you in the pool; everything else determines if you're admitted.
 
### The "Spike" Differentiator
Accepted applicants typically have one area where they are **nationally or internationally recognized**, or have done something so distinctive that it would be noted in an admissions officer's summary: *"This is the student who built a wildfire-prediction model that local fire departments actually use."*
 
### Authenticity Over Packaging
Stanford reads thousands of applications from students who founded nonprofits and did hospital volunteering. The distinction is whether:
- Your activities reflect a **genuine, multi-year passion** vs. résumé-building
- Your essays reveal **a specific, real person** vs. a carefully packaged brand
- Your recommenders describe **specific anecdotes** about your intellectual engagement vs. generic praise
### Intellectual Curiosity as a Differentiator
Rejected: Student lists 8 APs and high grades, generic interest in "medicine to help people."  
Accepted: Student lists 6 APs, also spent two summers independently reading computational biology papers, emailed a Stanford professor about a research question, and wrote an essay about a specific discovery in a paper that changed how they think.
 
### Character and "Fit"
Stanford explicitly rates "character/personal qualities" as **very important**. Admissions officers ask: *Will this student make our community better? Will they challenge their classmates? Will they contribute to Stanford in ways that can't be replaced by anyone else?*
 
### Red Flags That Hurt Applications
- Inconsistency between transcript rigor and stated passion
- Essays that read as written for Stanford specifically rather than authentically
- Activities that peak in senior year (looks like résumé-padding)
- Weak or generic teacher recommendations
- Test scores significantly below the middle 50% range (below 1480 SAT is a serious obstacle)
